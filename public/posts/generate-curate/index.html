Chaos

This class has been chaotic so far. This has probably been frustrating and confusing for some of you.

I try, as a teacher, to only make you frustrated or confused when it is a frustration or confusion that really happens in the workplace. I do this so you can find solutions for it in a safer environment (i.e. the classroom).

This course is about process and strategy.

The reason we have process is to create structure and organization, and therefore avoid confusion.

The reason we have Agile strategies is so that we're not so structured that we can't adapt. This is how we avoid frustration.

We began this course with very little structure, guided only by vague requirements, with no team infrastructure.

Using agile methodologies, we are gradually going to develop and iterate on methods that will make us increasingly more effective.

We are going to go through cycles of generation and curation - coming up with new ideas, trying them, and reflecting on how they worked for us.

You will find, in smaller groups, that you will each find a uniquely beneficial balance of structure and adaptability.

Listing pain points

Today we will continue to list pain points.

I expect you'll have thought of some new ones now that you're a few weeks into your new classes.

What are 'pain points' again?

As a reminder, pain points are things that are boring / repetitive or hard to remember. Stuff associated with process and strategy is usually boring and/or hard to remember, but so is a lot of work associated with development - syntax, running tests, boilerplates, server configuration, sourcing placeholder art, etc. etc. etc.

Luckily for us, computers are really good at doing repetitive stuff and remembering things.

We can automate literally anything a computer can do, creating triggers, sync'ing applications, passing in arguments, and/or chaining together commands.

The list of things computers can do is getting bigger every day. If your pain point involves finding artwork, or sending a text or email, or writing (either English or code), these are all things that can be automated (albeit with some costs associated beyond a certain point).

We can also have computers remember things for us in ways that are the most convenient for us - stored on a server that's accessible to us on all our devices, and/or on a collaborative platform, and/or in a format that's simple and easy to read and integrate with automations, and/or set up for automated reminders (both time- and location-based).

We'll take 20 minutes for you to individually list some of your own "pain points". These can be things that are currently painful, or things you know you probably should do, but aren't currently doing because it's too much work. This can be school stuff (from any course, past, present or future), or any tasks outside of school (that maybe you've neglected because you're schooling so hard).

Here are some of the pain points we listed Opens in a new window the first week.

Once you have some pain points listed, write out the steps involved in each painful task. Take note of anything that might change the next time you do this task. Take note of anything that you'd like to improve about how this task gets done.

Example:

Pain point: boilerplate folder structure
Description: It's hard to remember all the files and folders I should create for a project when I just want to start coding.
Steps:
1. Create a project folder
2. Create subfolders
3. Create boilerplate index.html, /js/script.js, /css/style.css
Note: this is for a static HTML site. Step 3 is slightly different for PHP projects.

Example:

Pain point: remembering due dates
Description: I can manually put due dates for assignments in my google calendar, but they're scattered all over, and it doesn't reflect how much time each assignment will take.
Steps:
1. Take a note in class
2. Manually add to Google Calendar
Note: I wish I could see how much time each task will take, and how many marks each task is worth. That way I'd know what to prioritize.

Okay, go for it:

  • Write out your pain points
  • Write out the steps involved in each pain point
  • Take note of anything that might change the next time you do this task
  • Take note of anything you'd like to improve about how this task gets done

Let's get ourselves into teams

Take a moment to reflect on your pain points. Choose one that feels like "low-hanging fruit" (i.e. an easy one to start with).

Which of the following solutions might be a good way to start thinking about addressing it?

  • Documentation (collaborative 'wiki'-style note-keeping in plain text/markdown)
  • Workflow automation (Github Actions, chaining together tools, testing, linting, etc.)
  • Boilerplating (creating reproducible "start points" for coding projects)
  • Task automation (having your own computer perform simple, repetitive tasks via shell scripting, Python, or similar)
  • Something else - literally any other strategy (that can ultimately be captured in Github).

Based on your answer, go to our Discord server, and join one of the five solution-* channels.

The people in that channel can form a team with you!

Teams must be >=5 and <10 people. It's up to you to decide who will be in your team.

Once we have our teams, we can begin the "Team forming" process.

Team forming

In your team, discuss, reach agreement on, and document the following:

  • Our team name is…
  • Our team will communicate* via…
  • Our team will track our features** using…

* You don't have to use Discord, but the course will continue to do so - oh, and I've given everyone the ability to create channels now, so you can totally have a channel just for your team.

** You don't have to use Trello, but your team can totally create a Kanban board just for your own use.

And remember - whatever you decide, you can totally change later. There will be plenty of opportunities to reflect on and update your process!

Sprint Planning

Now that we're in teams, we can start planning our first sprint.

Since we only work on this class while we're in this class, you'll need to base your sprint planning on the time we have available to us in this "sprint". This sprint will consist of one hour of working time next week, and 45 minutes the following week.

You will need to plan to deliver the following in your next sprint:

  • Any team administrative work, like setting up communication channels or feature tracking
  • Researching solutions (before you start actually coding/creating them)
